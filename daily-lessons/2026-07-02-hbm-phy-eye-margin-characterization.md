## Eye Diagram Fundamentals in HBM PHY Testing

An eye diagram is an overlay of all possible bit transitions accumulated over millions of UI (unit intervals), revealing the composite timing and voltage margin of a serial link. For HBM3 operating at 6.4 Gbps per pin (UI = 156.25 ps), the eye is generated by triggering an oscilloscope or ATE capture engine on the recovered clock and overlaying every received bit pattern.
Two critical dimensions define eye quality:
- **Eye width (horizontal margin):** Timing margin in ps or fraction of UI. JEDEC JESD235C requires a minimum open eye at the HBM die bump after package and interconnect de-embedding.- **Eye height (vertical margin):** Voltage margin in mV, bounded by noise floor, ISI, and supply-induced jitter.HBM2E (3.6 Gbps, UI = 277.8 ps) affords more timing margin than HBM3; validating to HBM3 specs therefore demands sub-picosecond ATE timing accuracy and calibrated interconnect de-embedding to the known-good reference plane.


## Bathtub Curve Construction and Gaussian Extrapolation

A bathtub curve plots BER as a function of strobe offset from the ideal sampling point. The horizontal bathtub sweeps timing offset across the UI; the vertical bathtub sweeps the decision threshold in mV. The resulting curve resembles a bathtub in cross-section — steep walls at the eye edges where BER rises rapidly, and a flat floor at the minimum achievable BER near the eye center.
Direct BER measurement at 10<sup>−15</sup> is impractical: at 6.4 Gbps, achieving one decade of statistical confidence requires ~3.3×10<sup>8</sup> seconds. Instead, engineers measure BER at the 1×10<sup>−9</sup> to 1×10<sup>−10</sup> level (reachable in seconds per offset point) and extrapolate using Gaussian tail fitting:
- **Dual-Dirac model:** Decomposes jitter into deterministic jitter (DJ, fixed amplitude, bounded) and random jitter (RJ, Gaussian, unbounded). BER is expressed as `BER = 0.5 × erfc((UI/2 − DJ/2 − t) / (RJ_rms × √2))`.- **Q-factor:** At a given BER, `Q = √2 × erfinv(1 − 2×BER)`. Plotting Q vs. offset on a linear scale reveals the Gaussian tails; extrapolating to Q ≈ 7.94 gives 10<sup>−15</sup>.- **Minimum eye width at 10<sup>−15</sup>:** Also called <em>BER eye width</em>, derived by subtracting both Gaussian tail extensions from the measured open eye.Commercial BERTScope instruments (Tektronix) and ATE BERT engines (Teradyne UltraFLEX HSD cards, Advantest T2000 DPPM option) perform this extrapolation natively, outputting the bathtub curve and fitted Gaussian parameters.


## BER Targets and JEDEC Specifications

JEDEC JESD235C (HBM3) mandates an unformatted raw BER floor of **1×10<sup>−15</sup>** per pin, measured at the receiver decision point inside the HBM die. This target drives system-level margin requirements because:
- HBM3 stacks use 1024-bit-wide interfaces (2 channels × 512 bits). At 1e-15 per pin, the aggregate channel BER is approximately 1.024×10<sup>−12</sup> — still below DRAM ECC correction capability.- HBM ECC (SECDED on 128+8-bit sub-channels) provides single-bit correction; BER above the target risks ECC saturation during burst accesses.Key JEDEC margin parameters from JESD235C:
- `tDQSQ` — DQS-to-DQ skew budget per channel, max ±5 ps (HBM3 Gen2)- `tQHS` — half swing timing, related to output driver linearity- `VREF` — reference voltage range 0.2×VDDQ to 0.8×VDDQ, centered at 0.5×VDDQ- Receiver sensitivity: minimum differential swing ΔV ≥ 100 mV at 1e-15 BERJEDEC leaves receiver BER testing methodology to implementers, but JESD209-5B (LPDDR5) appendices provide normative guidance on bathtub extrapolation that HBM vendors commonly adopt.


## Margin Budget Allocation Methodology

Total timing margin available at the receiver equals the raw eye width at the driver output minus all allocated losses across the signal path. The budget is expressed in UI fractions or ps, separated into deterministic and random components:
- **Package/substrate propagation:** 2–5 ps RMS for flip-chip BGA interconnect; characterised per-package SKU using TDR and S-parameter extraction.- **ISI (inter-symbol interference):** Dominant at HBM3 speeds; quantified from channel insertion loss S21 — typically 1–4 dB at Nyquist causing 10–30 ps DJ.- **Crosstalk:** Adjacent-pin NEXT/FEXT, modeled in IBIS-AMI simulations; budgeted as bounded DJ contribution of 3–8 ps per aggressor.- **PLL/DLL jitter:** Clock source phase noise integrated across the loop bandwidth; contributes RJ of 0.5–1.5 ps RMS at the HBM PHY.- **Supply-induced jitter (SIJ):** VDDQ transient coupling into I/O drivers; mitigated by on-package decoupling but residual contribution is 2–5 ps.- **Temperature/voltage (T/V) guard-band:** 5–10 ps additional margin held in reserve for worst-case junction temperature and VDDQ tolerance (±3%).The margin allocation is documented in the SI (signal integrity) budget spreadsheet, cross-referenced against ATE measurement results. Any budget overage triggers a root-cause investigation before production release.


## ATE Implementation: PHY Eye Margin Test Flow

On production ATE (Teradyne UltraFLEX with HSD-M cards, or Advantest T2000 with 64DSM modules), HBM PHY eye margin testing follows a structured flow:
- **Step 1 — Loopback configuration:** The HBM controller (in the logic die) is programmed via MRS (Mode Register Set) to loop DQ data back through the PHY receive path. This isolates PHY margin from DRAM array timing.- **Step 2 — PRBS pattern injection:** ATE drives a PRBS-23 pattern at the DQ inputs; PRBS-23 maximises ISI stress while providing sufficient transitions for CDR lock.- **Step 3 — Strobe sweep (timing bathtub):** The ATE timing edge is offset in 1–2 ps increments across ±40% UI from nominal center. BER is accumulated for ~100 ms per point (10<sup>−9</sup> floor at 6.4 Gbps = ~1.56×10<sup>9</sup> bits).- **Step 4 — Threshold sweep (voltage bathtub):** VREF DAC is swept ±80 mV from mid-rail in 2 mV steps; BER accumulated per point.- **Step 5 — Dual-Dirac fit and extrapolation:** ATE software fits Gaussian tails to each wall of the bathtub and reports RJ, DJ, and BER eye width at 10<sup>−15</sup>.- **Step 6 — Bin/pass criteria:** BER eye width ≥ spec limit (typically 0.3 UI after all budget allocations); voltage margin ≥ 150 mV. Parts failing either criterion are downbinned or retested at relaxed conditions to characterize the failure mode.Test time optimisation: production sites typically run a fast <em>go/no-go</em> BER check at ±25% UI (2–5 s per device) with full bathtub sweeps reserved for characterisation lots and correlation vehicles.


## Key Takeaways

- Bathtub curves enable 10^-15 BER validation through Gaussian extrapolation from short 10^-9 measurements, since direct 10^-15 testing is physically impractical at HBM3 speeds.
- Margin budget allocation must account for ISI, crosstalk, PLL jitter, supply-induced jitter, and T/V guard-band — any overage against the JEDEC JESD235C eye spec requires root-cause investigation before release.
- Production ATE PHY margin testing uses PHY loopback with PRBS-23, sweeps timing and voltage in fine steps, then applies Dual-Dirac fitting; pass/fail thresholds are typically 0.3 UI horizontal and 150 mV vertical at the 10^-15 BER contour.

## Additional Learning: 2D BER Contour Mapping for PHY Margin Corners

Beyond 1D bathtub curves, advanced HBM PHY characterisation employs 2D BER contour maps that sweep both timing offset and voltage threshold simultaneously, plotting iso-BER contours (e.g. 10^-9, 10^-12, extrapolated 10^-15) as closed ovals in the timing-voltage plane. The area enclosed by the 10^-15 contour is directly comparable to JEDEC's 2D eye mask, revealing asymmetric margin that 1D bathtubs miss — for example, a device with adequate horizontal margin but a voltage-timing interaction that degrades both simultaneously under supply droop. Instruments such as the Keysight J-BERT N4903B and Tektronix BERTScope BSA286C support 2D eye scan modes; on ATE, equivalent functionality is achieved by scripting nested timing/voltage sweeps with the BERT engine.

# AI Workload HBM Test Coverage: LLM Inference Bandwidth

*Sunday, Jun 28 2026*

## LLM Inference Memory Access Characteristics

Large language model (LLM) inference workloads stress HBM under access patterns fundamentally different from traditional HPC or graphics applications. The two dominant phases — **prefill** (prompt processing) and **decode** (token generation) — each place unique demands on HBM bandwidth and timing margins.
During prefill, attention computation over the input sequence creates near-sequential read streams of weight tensors with high spatial locality. During decode, the **KV-cache read** dominates: each new token requires reading all previously cached key-value pairs, producing a growing sequential-plus-scatter access pattern across HBM banks.
- **Arithmetic intensity:** Decode operations are heavily memory-bandwidth-bound, with effective arithmetic intensity often below 1 FLOP/byte on large models, making HBM bandwidth the sole performance limiter.- **Access granularity:** Transformer weight reads typically issue 256-bit or 512-bit bursts aligned to cache lines, well-matched to HBM burst length BL=4 and BL=8.- **Bank group utilisation:** KV-cache streaming can saturate specific bank groups, exposing `tREFab` and `tRFC` timing margin issues invisible to synthetic PRBS test patterns.

## HBM Bandwidth Specifications and AI Stress Targets

JEDEC JESD235C specifies HBM2E pin speeds up to 3.6 Gbps per pin, delivering 460 GB/s aggregate per 1024-bit stack. JESD238 (HBM3) extends this to 6.4 Gbps per pin and 819 GB/s per stack. AI accelerators typically co-package 4–8 HBM stacks, reaching aggregate bandwidths of 3.2–6.5 TB/s.
Test coverage must verify **sustained bandwidth efficiency** — actual achieved bandwidth divided by theoretical peak — remains above target under LLM-representative patterns. Synthetic sequential patterns routinely achieve ≥95%, but mixed read/write ratios and irregular KV-cache scatter can reduce efficiency to 75–80%, revealing real product margin.
- `tRCD` violations become more probable when scattered KV-cache reads force repeated bank activations within the same bank group.- `tFAW` (Four Activate Window) constrains burst-activation rates; LLM scatter patterns can violate tFAW budgets if the memory controller scheduler is not tuned for inference traffic.- Effective row-hit rate under decode workloads is typically 30–50%, far below the 80%+ assumed in traditional DRAM test coverage, making row-miss penalty accumulation a critical coverage gap.

## Bandwidth Stress Pattern Design for LLM Workloads

Effective ATE patterns for AI-workload HBM coverage must replicate three distinct traffic profiles: **streaming weight load**, **KV-cache scatter read**, and **output accumulation write**.
**Streaming Weight Pattern:** Sequential reads across the full HBM address space with BL=8, targeting 100% row-hit rate per bank. Establishes peak bandwidth baseline. Expected efficiency: ≥94% of theoretical peak at nominal VDD.
**KV-Cache Scatter Pattern:** Read addresses generated by a linear-feedback shift register (LFSR) with period 2^32−1, constrained to 4 KB-aligned blocks to simulate attention-head scatter across banks. This stresses bank-group arbitration and `tFAW` timing. Interleave 15% write traffic (output token accumulation) at uniform intervals to match decode-phase traffic mix.
**Prefill Burst Pattern:** Large sequential reads of 256 MB blocks with 4 KB stride, mimicking matrix-multiply weight access. Interleave 8-byte scatter reads every 64 accesses to model attention map lookups.
- Exercise all patterns at nominal voltage (VDD = 1.2 V for HBM3) and at ±5% margins.- Run at thermal steady-state: junction temperature ≥85 °C to expose thermally activated timing failures common in co-packaged HBM under sustained AI inference load.- Pattern duration: minimum 500 ms per pattern to capture `tREFab` refresh stalls under full bandwidth load.

## ATE Implementation: Pattern Resources and Timing Closure

Implementing AI-workload bandwidth patterns on ATE (e.g., Advantest T2000, Teradyne UltraFLEX) requires dedicated pattern memory for LLM-representative address sequences. LFSR-based address generators can produce pseudo-random streams with configurable polynomial taps sufficient to emulate KV-cache scatter without storing the full address sequence in pattern RAM.
- **Pattern depth:** A 2^20-word deep pattern memory at 128-bit bus width provides 16 MB of address coverage — sufficient for one KV-cache scatter epoch. Looping with LFSR seed rotation avoids periodic aliasing artifacts.- **Bandwidth measurement:** Use the ATE's built-in cycle counter and expected-data comparison: `BW = (words_verified × bus_width) / (cycle_count × tCK)`.- **Read/write interleave ratio:** Target 85:15 for decode phase; adjust to 70:30 for prefill-heavy characterisation.- **Refresh accounting:** Enable per-bank refresh tracking in the ATE timing set to verify `tREFab` (32 µs for HBM3) and `tRFC` (≈350 ns) are met under full bandwidth load without stall accumulation.At wafer sort, apply de-rating factors to bandwidth targets: parasitic capacitance on the exposed 1024-bit bus exceeds final-package conditions, typically reducing achievable bandwidth by 5–8% at probe. Fail limits at sort should be tightened accordingly to avoid shipping marginal die that pass probe but fail in 2.5D packages.


## Coverage Metrics and Pass/Fail Criteria

Define explicit, measurable coverage targets for AI-workload HBM test adequacy:
- **Peak bandwidth efficiency ≥ 92%** under streaming weight pattern at nominal conditions and 25 °C.- **Scatter pattern efficiency ≥ 78%** under LFSR KV-cache pattern — lower threshold acknowledges inherent bank-group conflicts in scatter access.- **Zero tRCD/tFAW violations** detected by the ATE timing-violation checker during a 500 ms scatter-pattern run at nominal VDD/temperature.- **Thermal bandwidth droop ≤ 5%**: compare streaming bandwidth at 25 °C versus 85 °C. Droop beyond 5% indicates marginal timing that will fail under sustained AI inference thermal conditions in the field.- **Refresh stall rate ≤ 2%**: fraction of cycles stalled on `tRFC`. Values above 2% indicate insufficient bandwidth headroom for production LLM serving workloads that require deterministic latency.Correlate ATE-measured bandwidth with hardware-in-the-loop (HIL) characterisation on actual AI accelerator boards. A ≥5% discrepancy between ATE and HIL bandwidth indicates pattern fidelity gaps or parasitic calibration errors requiring tester setup review before production release.


## Key Takeaways

- LLM inference has two distinct HBM stress phases — prefill (streaming weight reads) and decode (KV-cache scatter reads) — each requiring different ATE test patterns.
- KV-cache scatter patterns stress bank-group arbitration and tFAW timing in ways that traditional sequential PRBS patterns cannot reveal.
- ATE bandwidth stress patterns must target ≥92% efficiency for sequential workloads and ≥78% for scatter, verified at thermal steady-state (85 °C junction temperature).
- Refresh stall rate under full bandwidth load is a critical LLM-specific metric: values above 2% predict deterministic latency violations in production inference workloads.

## Additional Learning: Continuous Batching and Dynamic KV-Cache Bandwidth Variance

Modern LLM serving systems (e.g., vLLM, TensorRT-LLM) use continuous batching to interleave prefill and decode phases from multiple simultaneous requests. This creates a dynamically varying HBM traffic profile where the read-to-write ratio and spatial locality shift on a millisecond timescale. ATE stress patterns that assume a fixed 85:15 read-write ratio under-cover this dynamic regime; production-representative test coverage requires time-varying traffic profiles that alternate between prefill-heavy and decode-heavy patterns within a single test sequence, with transition rates matched to typical serving-system scheduling periods of 1–10 ms.
